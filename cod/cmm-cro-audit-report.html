Executive Summary

McKinsey Pyramid: Situation → Complication → Resolution

Situation

COD has a proven $100M+ business model with 6,000+ success stories and a masterclass that converts well once watched. The /cmm page is a free masterclass opt-in designed to capture leads for their coaching funnel. The business fundamentals are exceptionally strong.

Complication

The page is leaving significant conversion on the table. It uses a single-step form with a phone number field (known to kill 40-79% of completions), buries social proof below the fold, lacks any urgency mechanism, and presents a "How We" headline that positions the brand rather than the visitor. The page also fails to leverage COD's massive proof arsenal—6,000 clients, $100M revenue, Trustpilot 4.4/5 with 508 reviews, since 2013—in the hero section where it matters most.

Resolution

Seven high-impact changes, backed by 822+ CRO case studies and behavioral psychology research, can transform this page. The largest single lever: converting to a multi-step form (+86-214% completion). Combined with above-fold social proof, first-person CTA copy, and strategic urgency, we project a 40-80% lift in opt-in rate.

Critical Findings

Top 12 findings ranked by severity and projected impact. Each is backed by specific evidence from our 822+ CRO case study database.

Critical - Fix Immediately

#1 Single-Step Form Killing Mobile Conversions

Single-step form with all 3 fields showing at once. On mobile, this creates a wall of inputs that drives -40% completions vs desktop. Convert to 3-step: Step 1 (Name+Email), Step 2 (Phone+Goals), Step 3 (Commitment Q).

CF-004 +214% CF-092 +86-205% CF-093 F-050 +209%

#2 Phone Number Field Visible Upfront

Phone is the highest-friction field. Showing it in Step 1 creates immediate resistance. Move to Step 2 of multi-step form where commitment psychology kicks in.

CF-091 +79%

#3 Social Proof Completely Buried Below Fold

Trustpilot rating, "6,247 success stories," and media logos should be in the hero. Visitors make trust decisions in 50ms. The proof exists but nobody sees it before the form ask.

CF-008 +34% CF-045 +52%

#4 Zero Urgency on a Free Offer

No countdown, no limited spots, no next session time. For free content, "I'll come back later" is the conversion killer. Add genuine urgency: next masterclass session countdown.

Section 07

Behavioral Psychology Playbook

Seven models from Rory Sutherland and behavioral science, applied directly to /cmm. These aren't theory. Each maps to a specific page change.

APP-010

The Reframing Toolkit

Current: "Watch a masterclass" (passive consumption). Reframe: "Get the exact script that built a $100M business" (ownership + specificity). Reframing the action changes the perceived value.

APP-012

Costly Signaling

COD has invested $100M+ and 13 years. This IS the costly signal. Make it visible: "We've spent $100M testing this so you don't have to." The investment itself is the proof.

APP-008

Fake Urgency Destroys Trust

Don't add a fake countdown. DO add genuine urgency: next masterclass time, spots filled this week, or "limited to 500 free seats/month." Sophisticated audiences detect and punish manufactured scarcity.

APP-005

Strategic Friction

The phone number field IS friction but doesn't add value perception. Move it to Step 2 where it feels like "we're getting serious" rather than "give us everything upfront." Context determines whether friction helps or hurts.

APP-011

Specificity Beats Generality

"6,000+ success stories" should be "6,247 coaches transformed their business." Precision triggers the credibility heuristic. Our brains interpret exact numbers as measured, round numbers as estimated.

APP-023

The Doorman Audit

Add: "This masterclass is for coaches, consultants, and experts only. If you don't have expertise to sell, this isn't for you." Exclusion INCREASES perceived value. The velvet rope makes the room more desirable.

APP-020

Two-Step Commitment

Multi-step form creates micro-commitments. After entering name/email (Step 1), abandoning at Step 2 feels inconsistent. Cialdini's consistency principle: people want to finish what they start.
