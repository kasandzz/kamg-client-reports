Cold Email Booking -- Nurture Sequence

3 emails that bridge from cold email curiosity to excited call attendee. Therapist niche. FTC compliant.

Strategy Notes

  • Audience: Therapists/counselors who replied to Russ's cold email and booked a discovery call. They're curious but not committed.
  • Cold email promise: "I've helped thousands of therapists build premium programs around what they already know, so they're not tied to sessions."
  • Their mindset: Doing 1-on-1 work, want to learn about group programs, cautiously optimistic, some skeptical
  • Goal: Get them to actually show up excited, not just booked. Reduce no-shows by reinforcing their desire + giving them something to think about before the call.
  • Tone: Warm, personal from Russ. Conversational, not salesy. Match the cold email energy -- peer-to-peer, not guru-to-student.
  • FTC: Zero income claims. No "earn X" or "clients typically make..." -- we talk about structure, impact, and reach only.
  • Handoff: Russ sends the cold email, but the call is with a sales rep. Email 1 introduces the rep warmly with a punchy "don't tell them I said that" one-liner. Emails 2+3 use the rep's name so there's zero confusion at call time.

Why This Sequence Works

  • Bridges the gap: Cold email responders are curious but not invested. These emails build investment by getting them to think about their own program before the call.
  • "One thing" question: Giving them homework (pick one outcome to teach as a group) makes them mentally commit. People who do pre-work show up at 2-3x the rate.
  • Social proof without claims: "A lot of therapists we've worked with had the same starting point" is credible without being an income claim.
  • Anti-pitch framing: "This isn't a pitch call" and "If there isn't a fit, I'll tell you" reduce sales resistance. Cold email leads are more skeptical than ad leads.
  • Reschedule > cancel: Every email makes rescheduling easier than canceling. The 24h "don't ghost me" line gives psychological permission to reschedule without shame.
  • Group outcomes angle: "Group dynamics do things 1-on-1 can't" reframes the transition from "less personal" to "actually better for clients" -- which matters to therapists who care about client outcomes.
