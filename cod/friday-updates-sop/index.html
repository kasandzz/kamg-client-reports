The Philosophy · Read This First

This is not a status meeting.

Status meetings are where momentum goes to die. The reason this system works is because it forces every owner in the company to actually look at what they shipped this week, what they're shipping next week, and what's blocking them — and to say it out loud in front of the people who can help or hold them accountable.

80% of the value comes from the prep, not the meeting. The meeting is just the moment we all sync.

We were spinning too many plates. Everyone was busy. Nobody was sure what anyone else was doing. Things were falling through the cracks not because people weren't working hard, but because there was no rhythm — no point in the week where the company stopped, looked up, and said "okay, here's where we are."

The Friday Update fixes that. One time per week, every project owner reports against the same template, the team aligns, blockers get unblocked on the call, and we walk into the weekend knowing exactly what we're hitting Monday morning.

The Core Mindset

The week is not over until Friday's update is posted. The next week doesn't start Monday — it starts Friday afternoon when the plan is locked.

Part 1

The Mindset & Readiness

Before you show up to Friday Updates, you internalize these principles.

Own your projects like they're your business. You are not "assigned tasks." You are the CEO of every project you own. That means you know its status, you know what's blocking it, you know who you need to unblock it, and you walk into Friday with a clear answer to "where is this?" If you have to look at your Asana for the first time in the meeting, you've already failed the prep.

Update Asana before the meeting, not during it. Asana is the source of truth. The Friday Update is a review of what's already in Asana, not the place to retroactively log work. If a task isn't in Asana by Thursday end-of-day, it didn't happen as far as the team is concerned. This is how we build a real operating system instead of a verbal handoff culture.

Be brutally honest about status. No spin. No "almost done." No "I'll have it Monday" when you mean "I'll have it next Friday." The number one killer of weekly cadence is owners who soft-status to look good in the meeting and then quietly slip. Say the real status. Say the real blocker. Say the real deadline. The team can help you only if you tell the truth.

Bring a plan, not problems. A blocker without a proposed unblock is a complaint. A blocker with "I need 10 minutes with Matt to decide X" is a leadership move. Walk into Friday with proposed solutions to your own blockers and ask for the specific help you need. Don't dump.

Show up to other people's updates. You are not in the meeting only for your slot. You are there because what someone else owns probably touches what you own. When a project gets reviewed that isn't yours, listen — you might catch a dependency nobody flagged. The strongest teams cross-help in the meeting itself.

Part 2

Why Friday, Not Monday

Most companies do weekly updates on Monday. We do them Friday. There's a reason.

Friday updates close the week with truth. Monday updates are aspirational — everyone talks about what they're "going to do this week." That's planning, not reporting. Friday forces a real accounting of what actually happened in the last five days. Did you ship it or didn't you? No fudging it because Monday is so far away nobody remembers.

Friday updates lock the next week before the weekend. Once we leave the Friday call, the plan for the upcoming week is set. That means Saturday and Sunday you can actually relax — or get ahead — because you know exactly what Monday looks like. There's no "I'll figure out my week on Monday morning." Monday morning is execution time, not planning time.

Monday is for KPIs, Friday is for projects. This is the split. Friday we review what's in motion — projects, tasks, deliverables, deadlines, blockers. Tuesday morning we review numbers — cost per booked call, show rate, close rate, revenue, ad spend, pipeline. Different cadences, different brains, different meetings. Mixing them kills both.

Weekend buffer for word-smithing. If the Friday update surfaces something Russ needs to wordsmith, copy that needs review, or a decision that needs sleeping on — the weekend gives space for that. By Monday, the cleaned-up version is back in the team's hands ready to ship.

Part 3

The Asana Source-of-Truth Rule

If the project isn't in Asana, it doesn't exist. This is non-negotiable.

Every Active Project Must Have

  • A clear owner (one person — not two, not a team)
  • A current status (red / yellow / green or equivalent)
  • A target ship date (or explicit "no fixed date — exploratory")
  • Sub-tasks for any work older than 48 hours
  • Notes section updated within the last 7 days

Why this matters: In the Friday Update, we are not going to ask "what are you working on?" — we already know. We are going to ask "what's the status of [the project you own that's in Asana]?" If your project isn't in Asana, it can't be reviewed, which means it can't get unblocked, which means it dies in the dark.

The Thursday EOD Lock

By Thursday 5pm Pacific, every project owner has updated their Asana entries to reflect reality. This is the homework. The Friday meeting reads from Thursday's snapshot.

The Asana Color Protocol

Colors mean something. Use them precisely.

Green· On track, no help needed
Yellow· At risk, may need help
Red· Blocked or off-track
Grey· Backlog, not active
Done· Shipped
The Yellow Rule

A project cannot stay yellow for two consecutive Fridays. It either gets resolved back to green or it escalates to red. Yellow is a temporary state, not a permanent one.

Task Status Conventions

Within each project, every task gets one of these statuses:

  • Not Started — work hasn't begun
  • In Progress — actively being worked
  • Waiting On [Name] — handed off to someone else; specify who
  • Blocked — can't proceed without external input; specify what
  • Done — completed and verified by owner

"Waiting On" and "Blocked" are different. "Waiting On" means someone else owns the next move. "Blocked" means nobody owns the next move and we need to assign it in Friday's meeting.

Part 4

The Pre-Meeting Homework

Every owner does this on Thursday afternoon. No exceptions.

The Five-Question Prep Checklist

Before Friday's meeting, every owner answers these five questions in Asana, in their project's notes section:

  1. What shipped this week? Specific deliverables — be concrete. "Therapist workshop page outcomes-first rewrite — live as of Wed."
  2. What's in motion right now? Active work-in-progress with current status.
  3. What's the plan for next week? Specific deliverables you're committing to.
  4. What deadlines are coming up? Anything due in the next 14 days — internal or external.
  5. What's blocking you? Be specific about who or what you need.
The Standard

If someone reads your Asana notes cold on Friday morning, they should be able to fully understand the state of your project in under 60 seconds. If your update requires explanation in the meeting, your written prep was incomplete.

The "Specific or Don't Say It" Rule

Vagueness is the enemy of weekly cadence.

"Working on the LP rebuild."
"Therapist LP rebuild — outcomes block live, pain-point block removed, FAQ section in draft, expecting full ship by Tuesday EOD. Need Akari review on FAQ copy before push."
"Ads are going."
"Three new image ads launched Wed for therapist audience — CPM $42, CTR 1.8%, currently 4 calls booked at $310 CPBC. Killing the worst performer Friday, replacing with new angle Monday."
"I'll send it next week."
"Workshop email sequence v2 — sending draft to Russ for word-smith Monday morning; expect approved version live in automation by Wednesday."

Specificity is respect. It saves everyone else time.

The Deadline Declaration

For every commitment you make in Friday Updates, you state:

  • What: The specific deliverable
  • When: A date, not "next week" — Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday, etc.
  • Done means: What "shipped" looks like (live in GHL, in the team channel, in client's inbox, etc.)

If you can't state all three, you don't have a commitment — you have an intention. Intentions don't go on the board. Commitments do.

Part 5

The Five-Block Template

Every owner reports in this exact order in the meeting. No deviation, no preamble, no warm-up.

Block 1

Shipped This Week

60 seconds max

What did you actually finish this week? Specific deliverables, in plain language. If it's not live, it didn't ship — it's still in motion.

Block 2

In Motion

90 seconds max

What's actively being worked on right now? Where is each piece sitting? When does it ship?

Block 3

Next Week's Plan

60 seconds max

2–4 specific deliverables for next Friday. No more. Seven means you're lying to yourself or you don't have priorities.

Block 4

Deadlines · 14 Days

30 seconds max

Date-locked items. Client commitments, vendor deadlines, event dates, payment due dates.

Block 5

Blockers + Help

60 seconds max

What's stopping you, and what specifically do you need? Name names. Name decisions. Name the help.

Block 1 · Shipped This Week — Example

This week I shipped: (1) Therapist workshop page outcomes-first rewrite, live Wednesday. (2) New attorney workshop headline test, live Thursday. (3) Cleaned up the BigQuery validation checks for the dashboard MVP — Leo and I confirmed the data pipes are healthy. That's the win column.

Block 2 · In Motion — Example

In motion: (1) Cold email reply agent MVP — drafted Wednesday, training data scraped from GHL Thursday, expect first end-to-end test Monday. (2) Workshop/VIP email sequence v2 — draft sent to Russ for word-smith Friday morning, expecting reviewed version back Saturday. (3) Reactivation list for historical CPG leads — Krik building the list, I'll write the outreach sequence once I have it.

Block 3 · Next Week's Plan — Example

Next week I'm shipping: (1) Cold email reply agent MVP end-to-end working in our cloud — Tuesday. (2) Therapist LP A/B test for the new FAQ block — live Wednesday. (3) GHL post-enrollment SMS automation spec written up for Ryan and Matt F — Thursday.

Block 4 · Deadlines — Example

Deadlines: Eric expects Redstone weekly report EOD today. Lion's Pride event May 22 — promo emails need to go live by May 15. Volvic invoice resubmission due Monday.

Block 5 · Blockers — Example

Blockers: (1) Waiting on Matt F's call on whether we extend post-enrollment automation through GHL or pipe it through a separate Twilio integration — need 10 minutes with him this week. (2) Akari needs to greenlight the therapist FAQ copy before I push — flagging for her in the meeting now. (3) Need Russ to confirm if we're pulling Theo Ritter from the financing list before Friday's EOD report.
Time Math

Total max per person: 4–5 minutes. With 8 owners, that's 35 minutes of structured update + 15 minutes of cross-team discussion + 10 minutes of next-week alignment = 60-minute meeting cap.

Part 6

The Meeting Itself · Run of Show

This is how the meeting runs, every Friday, without deviation.

Pre-Meeting · Thursday 5pm Pacific

  • Every owner has updated Asana per Part 4
  • Meeting host (rotates monthly) confirms all updates are in
  • Any owner who hasn't updated gets a Slack nudge — your absence shows up in the meeting whether you do or don't

Meeting Opening · 5 minutes

Host opens with:

Alright — Friday Updates. Let's lock the week. We're going in this order: [list owners and projects]. Quick rules — five blocks per person, four minutes each, blockers go in the parking lot if they need more than 60 seconds of discussion, we hit the cross-team chat at the end.

No small talk. No "how was everyone's week." If someone wants to chat, that's before or after the meeting. The clock starts.

Owner Round Robin · 35–45 minutes

Each owner runs the five-block template. Host watches the clock — when someone's running long, gentle prompt: "Park that one — let's come back at the end."

The cardinal rule: When someone else is updating, you are listening. Not in Slack, not on email. If you catch a dependency, flag it ("Hold on — that intersects with the GHL automation I'm building"). If you don't catch anything, you're silent until your turn.

Parking Lot Resolution · 10–15 minutes

Host pulls the parked items and assigns them to the right pair or trio for after-meeting resolution.

Matt F and Kas — sync today at 2pm on the GHL/Twilio decision. Akari and Pedro — sync Monday on the therapist FAQ copy. Done. Moving on.

This is where the meeting earns its keep. Blockers that lived in DMs and email threads for a week get resolved in 90 seconds because the right people are on the same call.

Next-Week Lock · 5 minutes

Host summarizes:

  • Top three company-wide priorities for next week (not 12 — three)
  • Critical deadlines to flag for the team
  • Who's pairing with whom on blockers
  • What's at risk going into Monday

Everyone leaves the meeting with crystal clarity on what next week looks like and what their first move is Monday morning.

Meeting Close

Host closes with:

We're locked. Asana reflects what we just said. Everyone updates their entries by 6pm today if there's any change. See you Tuesday for stats.

That's it. No goodbye round, no "anything else?" Done.

Part 7

Project Status Conventions

How projects get talked about. Consistent vocabulary across the company.

The Status Sentence Format

Every project gets described in this format when called on:

[Project Name] — [Color/Status]. Last week: [what shipped]. This week: [what's in motion]. Next week: [what ships]. Blocker: [what's in the way, or 'none'].

Practice this until it's reflexive. Six seconds to deliver. Anyone on the team should be able to do this for any project they own without preparation.

The "Define Done" Rule

When committing to a deliverable, you must define what "done" means. Vague "done" is the silent killer of weekly accountability.

Examples of Well-Defined Done

  • "Therapist LP rebuild done = new version live at codworkshop.com/mc-therapist, GA4 confirms event firing, Slack screenshot posted in #brickellads"
  • "Cold email reply agent MVP done = end-to-end working in our cloud, processes 10 test inbound emails correctly, Russ has tested it himself"
  • "Workshop email sequence v2 done = all 5 emails live in GHL automation, first send fired, Akari has eyes-on confirmation"

Examples of Poorly-Defined Done

  • "Pretty much there"
  • "Just polishing"
  • "Should be good by next week"

If you can't define done, you can't ship.

One Throat to Choke

Every project has exactly one owner. Not two co-owners. Not a team. One person whose name is in the Asana owner field and who reports on it Friday.

You can have helpers. You can have collaborators. You can have approvers. But the owner is one person. That person is responsible for the project's status, the project's deadlines, and the project's eventual shipping. If they need help, they ask. They do not get to point at "we" when something slips.

Co-Leadership Edge Case

If a project genuinely needs co-leadership, split it into two projects with two owners and one explicit handoff point.

Part 8

Owner Accountability

The Friday Update is where soft expectations become hard accountability.

Commitment Made, Commitment Tracked

When you commit to a deliverable in Friday Updates, it goes into Asana as a task with:

  • Owner: you
  • Due date: the specific date you said
  • Done definition: what you said done looks like

Next Friday, the host pulls last week's commitments and you report against them. Shipped? Slipped? What happened?

This isn't about punishment. It's about pattern recognition. Are you a person who consistently delivers what you commit to? Or are you a person who consistently overcommits and slips? Both are data the team needs.

Slip Once → Diagnose. Slip Twice → Restructure.

If you slip a deliverable once, no blame. We diagnose: was the scope wrong? Was the deadline wrong? Was something blocking you that we didn't know about? Lessons learned.

If you slip the same deliverable twice in a row, we restructure. The work moves to a different owner, or it gets broken into smaller pieces with a smaller scope, or it gets killed because we admit it wasn't actually a priority.

What we do not do: pretend it's still happening for a third week in a row. Three weeks of slip on the same project is operational rot, and rot spreads.

The Public Commitment Effect

Saying "I will ship X by Wednesday" in front of eight teammates lifts your delivery rate. Private commitments hit 60% completion. Public commitments hit 94%. Use this dynamic on yourself deliberately — name the date, name the deliverable, in front of the room.

Part 9

Handling Blockers & Escalation

The meeting is the unblock factory. Use it.

The Three Blocker Categories

TypeLooks LikeResolution Path
Decision BlockerWaiting on someone to make a call. "Need Russ to greenlight the new headline."Resolve in the meeting if possible, otherwise schedule a 10-minute sync that day.
Resource BlockerNeed access, tools, money, or hands. "Need API access to Asana to wire up the cold email agent."Assign someone to make it happen this week.
Knowledge BlockerDon't know how to do the next step. "I don't know how to set up the Twilio webhook."Pair with someone who does, or budget time to learn it.

Each blocker type has a different resolution path. Naming the type makes the path obvious.

The Escalation Ladder

If a blocker survives three Friday Updates without resolution, it escalates.

1

Friday 1

Surfaced. Owner assigned. Help requested.

2

Friday 2

Still blocked. Reassigned or scoped down.

3

Friday 3

Russ steps in. Kill, pivot, or hard external deadline.

A blocker is not allowed to live as "still blocked" for four consecutive Fridays. That's the team failing the project. Kill it, fix it, or change owners.

Blockers That Are Actually Decisions

Sometimes "I'm blocked" really means "I don't want to make the call." The meeting catches this. If someone says they're blocked on a decision they're empowered to make themselves, the host calls it: "That's not a blocker — that's a call you can make. Make it by Monday."

The Tuesday KPI Counterpart

Friday is for projects. Tuesday is for numbers. They are different meetings on different days with different rules.

This SOP governs Friday. The Tuesday KPI Review has its own SOP, but the high-level split is:

MeetingCadenceFocusOutput
Friday UpdatesWeekly · Fri AMProjects, tasks, owners, deadlines, blockersNext week's plan locked
Tuesday KPIsWeekly · Tue AMCost per booked call, show rate, close rate, revenue, spend, conversionTactical adjustments to ad spend, funnel mechanics, sales staffing
Daily Check-InDaily · Mon–Fri AMSame-day blockers, in-the-moment escalationsUnblocks before the day starts

The reason for the split: project review requires patience and detail. KPI review requires speed and pattern recognition. Mixing them means projects get rushed and numbers get glossed. Keep them separate.

Part 11

Failure Modes to Avoid

These are the patterns that kill weekly cadence. Know them. Don't do them.

1. The Pre-Meeting Update Skip

Owner shows up Friday without Asana updated; uses meeting time to retroactively log work.
Fix: Owner does prep before Friday or they get skipped in the round-robin and update on their own time after.

2. The Soft Status

"Almost done." "Just polishing." "Should ship next week." These are not statuses — they are evasions.
Fix: Define done. Name the date. If you can't, the project is in trouble and we need to talk about it.

3. The 12-Minute Monologue

One owner monologues through their entire week instead of running the five-block template.
Fix: Host enforces the time box. At 4 minutes, you're done — anything else goes in the parking lot.

4. The Silent Listener

Someone goes quiet during others' updates, misses a dependency that affects their own work, finds out Tuesday.
Fix: Attendance means attention. If you can't be present, send your update in writing and skip the call.

5. The Recurring Yellow

A project sits at yellow for three Fridays in a row. Nobody resolves it back to green or escalates to red.
Fix: No yellow lasts two Fridays. Resolve it or escalate it.

6. The Phantom Project

A project gets reported on every Friday but never has a concrete ship date and never seems to move.
Fix: Force a real ship date or kill the project. The team's energy is not infinite.

7. The Cross-Talk Spiral

Two owners debate an overlapping dependency for 15 minutes while six other people sit there.
Fix: Park it. Sync after the meeting. The rest of the team doesn't need to watch you negotiate.

8. The Strategy Drift

Someone starts proposing a new big initiative in Friday Updates.
Fix: Strategy conversations happen in dedicated strategy sessions, not here. Friday is for what already exists.
Part 12

Friday Update Etiquette

The unwritten rules that make the meeting actually work.

  • Be on the call on time. If you're late, you don't get to update first when you arrive. You go to the back of the queue.
  • Cameras on. Video changes the energy. We're a remote team — body language is the only signal we have. Don't audio-only into a weekly company-wide call.
  • No multitasking visible. If you need to handle something during the meeting, step off mute and say so. "Sorry team, I've got a client fire — going off video for two minutes." Honest interruption beats silent absence.
  • Name names, not roles. "Akari needs to greenlight the FAQ copy" beats "we need someone on the copy side to approve it." Specificity routes faster.
  • Celebrate publicly. When someone ships something big, the team acknowledges it. Quick. Specific. Not a five-minute roast. Just "Nice work on the therapist LP — that was a hard one." Then move on.
  • Handle conflict offline. If someone's late on something you're depending on, do not call them out in the meeting. Sync after. The meeting is for status, not for accountability theater.
  • Take notes in your own doc. The host or scribe captures decisions and assignments. Everyone else takes their own notes for their own action items. Don't expect the host to spoon-feed you what you committed to.
