Connecting Behavioral Data to Conversions

PostHog Behavioral Intelligence for Clients On Demand

What We're Doing

We're connecting PostHog's behavioral tracking (what people do on our pages) to Hyros and the rest of the data warehouse. This gives us one thing we don't have today: the ability to see exactly what someone did on our pages, how they engaged with the workshop, and then link that behavior directly to whether they converted.

Hyros tells us which ad generated a sale. PostHog tells us what happened between the click and the conversion. Right now those are two separate worlds. We're joining them.

Installation is simple. 2 small code snippets pasted into GHL page footers. Takes about 5 minutes. Nothing changes for visitors.

What Hyros Does vs. What PostHog Does

These tools are not competitors. They answer different questions.

QuestionHyrosPostHog
Which ad generated this sale?YesNo
What's our ROAS by ad set?YesNo
Which payment came from which traffic source?Yes (server-side, ~98% accuracy)No
What did someone do ON the landing page?NoYes: heatmaps, scroll depth, click tracking, session recordings
Which section of the page do buyers read vs skip?NoYes: section-level attention data
What do non-buyers do differently from buyers?No (only tracks converters)Yes: tracks every visitor

In short: Hyros is the attribution layer (ad to sale). PostHog is the behavior layer (what happens on the page). Together in BigQuery, they complete the picture.

Workshop attendance and watch duration will be tracked through AEvent's own analytics, which feeds into the same BigQuery warehouse per person. So every data point, whether from PostHog, AEvent, Hyros, GHL, or Stripe, lands in one place tied to a single email.

What This Unlocks

Which Parts of the Landing Page People Actually Read

PostHog records heatmaps and session recordings on every page automatically. This means we can see:

  • Scroll depth by buyer vs non-buyer. Do people who purchase the $27 ticket scroll past the testimonials? Do they read the FAQ? Or do they stop at the value stack and buy immediately? If 80% of buyers never scroll past Section 6, everything below it is wasted real estate.
  • What people click on that is not a button. Dead clicks reveal what visitors expect to be interactive. If 200 people per week click on "Russ has helped 6,000+ clients" expecting more detail, that is a signal to make it a link or expand the proof.
  • Rage clicks and u-turns. When someone clicks the same spot 5 times and then leaves, PostHog flags it. This catches broken elements, confusing layouts, and friction points invisible to any other tool.
  • Section-by-section attention. PostHog shows exactly how long visitors pause on each section. If the "Is This You?" pain-point section holds attention for 12 seconds but the "Before/After" table gets skipped in 2 seconds, that tells us which emotional triggers are landing and which are not.

A VWO study across 5,000+ experiments found that heatmap-informed landing page redesigns improve conversion rates by 20-30% on average. Hyros can tell you the landing page converted. PostHog shows you why (or why not).

The Behavior of Non-Buyers (The 75-84% Invisible to Hyros)

Hyros only tracks people who trigger a payment event. That means it is completely blind to:

  • The visitors who land on the registration page but don't register
  • The people who register but don't buy the $27 ticket
  • The 75-84% of ticket buyers who never book a call
  • Workshop attendees who watch the whole thing and then disappear

These are the biggest leak zones in the funnel, and they are invisible to every tool except PostHog. Session recordings of non-bookers will show us whether they got confused by the booking flow, got distracted, or simply weren't compelled by the offer.

The Behavioral Profile of Buyers vs Non-Buyers

Once PostHog identifies users by email and we join that with Hyros attribution data in BigQuery, we can build behavioral fingerprints. Not demographics. Behavior.

  • Pre-purchase browsing patterns. Did they visit 3 times before buying? Did they spend 4 minutes reading testimonials? Did they watch the hero video? Hyros sees one event (the purchase). PostHog sees every session leading up to it.
  • Speed-to-action. Buyers who purchase within 60 seconds of landing behave differently from those who come back the next day. Speed is a strong signal of buyer temperature.
  • Content consumption patterns. Which landing page sections hold their attention? Which do they skip? Do attorney buyers behave differently from therapist buyers on the same page?
  • Hesitation signals. If a group of buyers all pause on the pricing section for 30+ seconds, that is price sensitivity. If they all skip the "Not For You" section, they already self-selected.

The practical profile looks like this:

"Your highest-value buyers (those who enroll at $8K+) share these traits: they arrive from profession-specific ad sets (not broad), they read the full landing page (scroll depth 85%+), they purchase within the first visit, they watch 90+ minutes of the workshop, and they book a call within 4 hours of finishing. They are most likely to arrive on Tuesday or Wednesday between 10am and 2pm."

That profile is built from actual behavioral data across hundreds of buyers. Hyros confirms the revenue. PostHog provides the behavior that explains it.

How We Apply This Data Practically

Behavioral profiles drive concrete decisions that Hyros data alone cannot inform.

Ads optimization. When we combine PostHog behavioral data (which page sections buyers read) with Hyros attribution (which ad set they came from), we can build audience profiles that go deeper than "therapist vs attorney." We can see that therapists who spend 30+ seconds on the credibility section close at 2x the rate of those who skip it. That informs ad creative: lead with Russ's credentials for therapist audiences. Meta's own research (2024) shows that advertisers who feed first-party behavioral signals back into targeting see 20-35% CPA improvement.

Landing page optimization. Session recordings show that attorneys spend 2x longer on the credibility section but skip "Is This You?" entirely (they already know their problem). That means attorney-specific pages should lead with Russ's credentials and cut the agitation copy. Unbounce's 2023 Conversion Benchmark Report found that pages matched to visitor intent convert 2-4x better than generic pages. Hyros tells us attorneys convert well. PostHog shows us why, and how to make them convert better.

Workshop content optimization. If watch-duration data (from AEvent, centralized in BigQuery) shows 60% of non-bookers drop off at the 45-minute mark, we know exactly where to place a re-engagement hook. Cross-referencing that with PostHog's on-page behavior before the workshop tells us whether the dropout is about content fatigue or whether those people were never engaged to begin with.

Sales routing. When we can see the full on-page journey of every person who books a call, we can score them before the call happens. Someone who read every section, bought VIP, watched 120 minutes, and booked same-day is a hot lead. Route them to Dorian (25% close rate). Someone who skimmed the page, bought standard, watched 20 minutes, and booked 5 days later needs a different approach. HubSpot's 2024 Sales Enablement Report found that behavioral lead scoring increases close rates by 30%.

Retargeting precision. People who watched 90+ minutes of the workshop but did not book a call are the warmest retargeting audience possible. They trust Russ, they understand the offer. They just need a nudge. This audience segment cannot be built from Hyros data because Hyros does not track workshop behavior. AdRoll's data shows precisely-defined retargeting converts 70% higher than broad retargeting.

Email/SMS sequences. When we know that the average time between ticket purchase and workshop attendance is 36 hours, but same-day attendees book at 2x the rate, every reminder sequence can be calibrated to compress that gap. Data-driven timing instead of guesswork.
