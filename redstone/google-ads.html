01
Top priority for Month 2

The conversion tracking is broken — we're under-counting your leads.

Every conversion tracking pixel in your Google Ads account has been flagging "needs attention" since launch. In plain English: the wires that tell Google "a quote was requested" never connected properly.

That means the 6 leads figure above is almost certainly low. Your real number is higher — we just can't prove which clicks turned into quote requests, and Google can't optimise toward something it can't measure.

What we're doing: Rehan is rebuilding the tracking from scratch using PostHog (a more reliable tool than the previous setup). Targeted live by 24 April. Once it's live, we'll see Month 2's true cost-per-lead inside a week.

02

10,164 changes — almost all of them adding "do not show ads for this" rules.

The biggest job in Month 1 was teaching Google what not to show your ads for. Daniel grew your negative-keyword list from 3,409 → 7,044 — that's 3,635 new "do not show" rules added in 31 days.

Why so many? Google has changed how keyword matching works. Even when we tell it "only show ads for 'precision CNC machining Mexico'," it now also shows ads for vaguely related searches like 'machine shop hobby' or 'CNC for sale' — people who will never buy from Redstone. Daniel's job is to spot those wasted impressions every day and block them one by one.

It's tedious work, but it's the only way to clean up the traffic. And it's already working — which leads to the next number.

03
Trending up

Click-through rate jumped 34% in 4 weeks, despite Easter.

Click-through rate (CTR) is the cleanest signal that traffic quality is improving. The more relevant the people seeing your ads, the more of them click. Here's the trajectory:

Week 1 (10 Mar)
3.89%
Easter week
4.42%
Easter-adjusted
5.23%

Easter is the second-worst week of the year for Google Ads (after Christmas) — B2B traffic always drops. So the raw 4.42% in Easter week is actually equivalent to ~5.23% in a normal week. Either way, the line is moving the right direction. Month 2 starts with all that negative-keyword groundwork already paid for.

04

India + Mexico now have their own campaign — they were getting starved.

When we launched, your India and Mexico location ad groups were sitting inside the main campaign — and getting zero impressions. The broader generic terms ("CNC manufacturing", "sheet metal fabrication") were eating the entire daily budget before the location ads got a chance to run.

Daniel built them out as a separate campaign with their own dedicated budget. Now they actually run. The catch: location-targeted clicks cost more.

Main campaign — cost per click
$4.70
Generic CNC / sheet metal terms
India + Mexico — cost per click
$9.87

Location clicks are more than 2× the price. That's the cost of buyers who are specifically searching for "CNC machining Mexico" instead of just "CNC machining." They're more qualified — but if they land on a generic page, we waste the click. That's why dedicated India and Mexico landing pages are now a Month 2 priority (see Section 7).

05

We stopped showing your ads to 18–24 year olds.

The 18–24 age bracket was the worst-performing audience in the account by every metric we track. They click but they don't buy — they're not making sourcing decisions for industrial parts. Excluded mid-month. Frees up budget for the actual buyers (engineers and sourcing managers, typically 30–60).
